Ernest Hemingway

Hemingway's Life

Ernest Hemingway was an American journalist and writer, who won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1954 for his 1952 novel The Old Man and the Sea. His distinctive writing style had a strong influence on 20th-century fiction and many of his works are considered classics of American literature.

He was born on 21 July 1899 in Oak Park, Illinois. He took a job as a journalist for The Kansas City Star and then volunteered as a driver for the Red Cross in Italy during the First World War. Stationed on the Austrian front, he was seriously wounded by mortar fire, but continued to evacuate Italian soldiers to safety, later being awarded the Italian Silver Medal of Bravery for this action.

After the war, Hemingway moved back to America for a short period, and married Hadley Richardson before moving to Paris, and working as a foreign correspondent for the Toronto Star. In Paris, Hemingway met many influential writers and artists such as Gertrude Stein, Ezra Pound, James Joyce, Picasso and Joan Miro, and published his first books of poems and short stories Three Stories and Ten Poems. His first novel, The Sun Also Rises which is often recognised as his best work, was published in 1926. It draws on his experience of the bullfighting festival of San Fermin in Spain, which he attended with a group of American and British expatriates.

In 1927, Hemingway divorced Richardson and married Pauline Pfeiffer, and in 1928, they moved back to America, settling in Key West, Florida. From there, he wrote A Farewell To Arms which was published in 1929. He undertook many fishing trips in the Caribbean, and hunting expeditions to Wyoming and Kenya, and published further books, The Green Hills of Africa and To Have and Have Not.

Hemingway travelled to Spain in 1937 to report on the Civil War. On his return he divorced Pfeiffer and married Martha Gelhorn, moving to Cuba and renting a farm, Finca Vigia just outside of Havana, where he wrote For Whom the Bell Tolls.

From 1944 to 1945 Hemingway was in Europe, covering the war, and was present at the Normandy landings and liberation of Paris. He was awarded the Bronze Star for bravery. Hemingway divorced Gelhorn and after the war married Mary Welsh, moving back to Cuba. In 1945, he was involved in a car accident, sustaining a broken knee and head injuries, and started to suffer from various health issues. He wrote Across the River and Into the Trees in 1950 and The Old Man and the Sea in 1952, the latter winning him the Pulitzer Prize and then the Nobel Prize for Literature.

While on safari in East Africa in 1954, Hemingway was in a serious aircraft accident, sustaining serious head injuries. The next day, while being flown out to Entebbe in Uganda for medical treatment, the plane he was traveling in exploded on take-off, leaving him with further injuries and severe burns. His health deteriorated as a result and he became a heavy drinker.

In 1956, he returned to Europe and on discovering a cache of his old manuscripts at the Ritz, wrote A Moveable Feast, about his time in Paris in the 20s, and worked on The Garden of Eden, Islands in the Stream and True at First Light, all of which were published posthumously.

After the Invasion of the Bay of Pigs in 1961, Hemingway had to leave Cuba for good after his home was seized by the Castro government. He moved to Ketchum, Idaho, where he had bought a property some years earlier.

His physical and mental health deteriorated rapidly in 1961, and he ended his own life on 2 July 1961.
